# Quantitative Research Report

## Executive Summary
This event-study tested whether Tesla (TSLA) exhibits negative abnormal returns in the 1–5 trading days following Elon Musk tweets classified as negative (sentiment ≤ −0.2). The final test applied strict event filters (non-overlapping tweets since 2015, combined engagement ≥ 50,000, earnings/corporate-action windows excluded), winsorized returns (1st/99th percentiles), and controlled for market return and prior 20-day realized volatility. Results are weak: using 13 analyzed events the backtest produced a total return of −30.48%, an annualized return of −68.64%, Sharpe ratio −4.248, and max drawdown −39.52%. Event-study cumulative abnormal returns (CARs) across 1–5 day windows were small and statistically insignificant; sentiment subgroup samples were too small for reliable inference (negative n=2, positive n=4). Verdict: WEAK — the hypothesis as tested is not supported.

## Research Hypothesis
Final hypothesis tested (precise): TSLA exhibits statistically significant negative abnormal returns over the 1–5 trading days following Elon Musk tweets classified as negative (sentiment score ≤ −0.2 by a validated NLP model), restricting to non-overlapping tweets since 2015 with combined engagement (likes + retweets) ≥ 50,000, excluding earnings and corporate-action windows, compared against matched non-tweet control days while controlling for concurrent S&P 500 (^GSPC) returns and prior 20-day realized volatility. Analysis required at least 40 events; returns were winsorized at the 1st/99th percentiles and significance was assessed by non-parametric bootstrap/permutation tests (α = 0.05).

Sub-questions:
- Is mean cumulative log return of TSLA over 1–5 trading days post-tweet significantly negative vs matched non-event days?
- Does a market-adjusted CAR (r_TSLA − β·r_SPY) show a significant negative effect?
- Does tweet sentiment predict the sign/magnitude of 1–5 day CAR?
- Are TSLA trading volume and realized volatility elevated in the 1–5 days after tweets compared to the 5 days prior?

Assets analyzed: TSLA and ^GSPC. Timeframe: 2010-01-01 to 2024-01-01.

## Methodology
- Event selection: Elon Musk tweets (since 2015), sentiment scored with validated NLP model; negative defined as sentiment ≤ −0.2. Events filtered for non-overlap, combined engagement ≥ 50,000, and exclusion of earnings/corporate-action windows.
- Outcome variables: log returns of TSLA, 1–5 day cumulative log returns, market-adjusted CAR using SPY-based market model (CAR_t = r_TSLA,t − β·r_SPY,t), 5-day realized volatility, and trading volume.
- Controls: concurrent S&P 500 returns and prior 20-day realized volatility in regression specifications.
- Data processing: returns winsorized at 1st/99th percentiles.
- Statistical inference: matched non-event control days for direct comparison; coefficients and differences tested using non-parametric bootstrap/permutation tests (α = 0.05).
- Planned sample size (final hypothesis): ≥ 40 events. Actual trades/events analyzed: 13.

## Results
Key backtest outputs (exact):
- Trades analyzed (events): 13
- Win rate: 0.5385
- Total return: −30.48%
- Annualized return (reported): −68.64%
- Sharpe ratio: −4.248
- Max drawdown: −39.52%
- Event-study CARs (1–5 day windows): small in magnitude and not statistically significant (p-values > 0.05 across windows).
- Sentiment subgroup sizes: negative n = 2, positive n = 4 (insufficient for reliable subgroup inference).
- Regression coefficients on tweet indicator and sentiment: small; no reported statistically significant coefficients.
- Volume and realized-volatility comparisons: no significant increases in the 1–5 day post-tweet window vs prior 5 days (high p-values).

## Statistical Analysis
- Power and sample size: the final analysis required ≥ 40 events but only 13 events met the filters; this yields very low statistical power and high susceptibility to outliers. The small number of events undermines robustness of bootstrap/permutation inference.
- Significance: CARs and adjusted regressions returned p-values above α = 0.05; no effect was statistically distinguishable from zero in the 1–5 day horizon.
- Effect heterogeneity: sentiment subgroup analysis is unreliable due to extremely small group sizes (negative n=2, positive n=4). Any estimated differences across sentiment groups are not credible.
- Risk-adjusted performance: Sharpe = −4.248 indicates poor risk-adjusted outcomes; win rate >50% but average returns per trade negative, consistent with a pattern of small gains and larger losses.

## Risk Analysis
- Economic risk: large negative total return (−30.48%) and large max drawdown (−39.52%) imply material downside if one attempted a trade strategy based on these signals.
- Statistical risk: severe under-sampling (13 vs planned 40 events); high sensitivity to individual events; non-significant results likely reflect noise rather than a genuine effect.
- Model risk: potential selection and look-ahead biases were not fully ruled out — e.g., tweet selection mechanics, filtering rules, and event overlap procedures require independent audit.
- Data risk: winsorization mitigates extreme-return influence but with only 13 events outliers can still dominate results.

## Conclusion
The hypothesis that Tesla exhibits statistically significant negative abnormal returns over 1–5 trading days following Elon Musk tweets (as restricted and controlled in the final specification) is not supported by the analysis. The empirical evidence is weak: CARs across tested windows are small and statistically insignificant, regression adjustments show no meaningful coefficients, volume/volatility effects are non-significant, and risk-adjusted performance is poor (Sharpe = −4.248; total return −30.48%). The analysis was underpowered (13 events vs required 40) and sensitive to a few large losses. What was learned: strict filtering reduced sample size below practical inference thresholds; signals—if present—are small relative to noise and are not reliably exploitable in the tested design.

Recommendations: relax overly restrictive event filters to reach adequate sample size, run full robustness checks (outlier removal, alternative sentiment thresholds, alternative matching methods), provide full p-value/CI reporting for regression coefficients, and audit tweet selection to eliminate look-ahead bias before concluding on economic exploitable effects.

## Appendix: Hypothesis Evolution
1. Original: "Tesla stock drops in the week after Elon Musk tweets."
   - Why refined: too vague (no sentiment, no filters, no controls). Needed operationalization to be testable.

2. Iteration 2: Add sentiment classification (positive/neutral/negative) using an NLP model and test 1–5 day returns by sentiment group.
   - Why refined: sentiment directionality hypothesized to drive effects; initial group tests showed noisy results and imbalance.

3. Iteration 3: Add event filters — require non-overlapping tweets, restrict to tweets since 2015, require combined engagement ≥ 50,000, and exclude earnings/corporate-action windows.
   - Why refined: aim to focus on high-engagement, independent events and remove confounding corporate events. This substantially reduced sample size.

4. Final (Iteration 4): Add controls (concurrent ^GSPC returns, prior 20-day realized volatility), winsorize returns at 1st/99th percentiles, specify market model CAR using SPY in regressions, and plan to use non-parametric bootstrap/permutation inference with a minimum of 40 events.
   - Why refined: to produce a robust, market-adjusted test and control for volatility and extreme returns. In practice only 13 events met the filters, so the final planned test was underpowered.
