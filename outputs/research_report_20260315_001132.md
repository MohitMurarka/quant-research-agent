# Quantitative Research Report

## Executive Summary
This study tested whether S&P 500 January returns are positive on average, with a final focus on the pooled mean January total return conditional on a weak December (prior-Dec <= −1.5%). We analyzed dividend‑adjusted monthly total‑return series for SPY and ^GSPC from 2000‑01‑01 to 2024‑01‑01 (n = 23 January observations in the backtest). The conditional comparison produced a small observed mean difference (~0.00664) but was not statistically significant: one‑sided bootstrap p = 0.3787 and bootstrap 95% CI = (−0.03270, 0.04595). Other population and nonparametric tests were likewise non‑significant (January mean one‑sided p ≈ 0.4884; binomial positive count 11/23, p = 1.0, CI 0.268–0.694; Mann–Whitney p = 0.7583). Performance metrics are weak (Sharpe = −0.168, total return = 0.63%, win rate = 47.83%, max drawdown = −16.9%). Final verdict: WEAK — the hypothesis is not supported by the present evidence.

## Research Hypothesis
Final hypothesis tested (pre‑specified): The pooled mean January total return of the S&P 500 (SPY and ^GSPC monthly total‑return series) is greater than zero conditional on the prior December total return being <= −1.5%. Testing used a one‑sided permutation/bootstrap approach with a bootstrap 95% CI, a pre‑specified 5‑year out‑of‑sample (OOS) holdout, and an acceptance rule requiring the pooled conditional sample to have ≥30 observations and ≥80% power to detect a 1.0% monthly effect size.

Ancillary sub‑questions: (1) overall January mean > 0; (2) fraction of positive January returns > 0.5; (3) January return distribution vs other months (nonparametric); (4) relationship between January ETF volume and January returns (correlation and simple regression, with control for prior month).

Assets analyzed: SPY and ^GSPC; timeframe 2000‑01‑01 to 2024‑01‑01.

## Methodology
- Data: dividend‑adjusted month‑end total returns (last trading day to last trading day) for SPY and ^GSPC (2000–2024).
- Primary test: pooled conditional comparison of January returns when prior‑December return <= −1.5% vs unconditional/other cases, using a one‑sided permutation/bootstrap test for the mean and bootstrap 95% confidence intervals.
- Secondary tests: one‑sample t for January mean > 0 (reported effect size Cohen’s d), binomial test for fraction positive, Mann–Whitney U comparing January to other months, correlation and OLS regression of January returns on January volume change (SPY/VOO) controlling for prior‑December return.
- Robustness: pre‑specified 5‑year OOS holdout applied. Pre‑specified acceptance criteria included minimum pooled conditional sample size (≥30) and power threshold (≥80% to detect 1.0% monthly effect).
- Backtest reporting: performance metrics computed (Sharpe, total return, max drawdown, win rate, trades).

## Results
Key numerical outcomes:
- Observations/trades analyzed: 23 January observations.
- Conditional sample split: prior‑Dec < 0: n = 8; prior‑Dec ≥ 0: n = 15.
- Observed pooled mean difference (conditional): ≈ 0.00664.
- One‑sided bootstrap p (conditional test): 0.3787.
- Bootstrap 95% CI for mean difference: (−0.03270, 0.04595).
- Overall January mean test one‑sided p ≈ 0.4884; Cohen’s d ≈ 0.0062.
- Binomial positive‑count: 11/23 positive Januaries; binomial p = 1.0; 95% CI for proportion: 0.268–0.694.
- Mann–Whitney (January vs other months): p = 0.7583; median January return lower than median of other months.
- Out‑of‑sample (last 5 years) conditional test: p ≈ 0.2883 (non‑significant).
Performance metrics:
- Sharpe ratio: −0.168
- Total return: 0.0063 (0.63%)
- Win rate: 0.4783 (47.83%)
- Max drawdown: −0.169 (−16.9%)

## Statistical Analysis
The conditional effect size is small (~0.66 percentage points) and its bootstrap 95% CI includes zero, so we cannot reject the null of no positive conditional January mean. The one‑sample January mean and the binomial test for positive frequency are both non‑significant and the nonparametric Mann–Whitney shows no stochastic dominance of January over other months. The OOS check did not provide support either. A regression signal suggesting a negative coefficient on prior‑December return (i.e., more negative Dec associated with higher Jan) emerged in exploratory models, but given n = 23 and the conflicting bootstrap/nonparametric results this is not robust evidence. Multiple comparisons and small/imbalanced conditional cell sizes (n = 8 vs 15) further erode confidence in marginally significant coefficients.

## Risk Analysis
- Statistical risk: low power—conditional sample size is small and does not meet the pre‑specified ≥30 observation threshold, increasing Type II error risk for small effects.
- Economic risk: strategy performance is poor (negative Sharpe, low absolute return, drawdown −16.9%).
- Data/model risk: pooling SPY and ^GSPC reduces idiosyncratic noise but may introduce dependence; choice of threshold (−1.5%) and log‑return vs arithmetic returns may materially affect results.
- Multiple testing/fishing risk: many subtests were run; isolated marginal findings should be treated as exploratory until pre‑registered and replicated.

## Conclusion
The pre‑specified final hypothesis is not supported by the available evidence. The conditional pooled mean January return (prior‑Dec <= −1.5%) is small and statistically indistinguishable from zero (one‑sided bootstrap p = 0.3787; 95% CI includes zero). Secondary population tests (January mean, positive fraction, distributional comparisons) are non‑significant and performance metrics are weak (Sharpe = −0.168, total return 0.63%, win rate <50%). Given small/imbalanced samples, low power, and non‑significance in OOS checks, the claim that January returns are reliably positive conditional on a weak December cannot be accepted. Recommendations: either collect more data (longer series or additional instruments), pre‑specify/coalesce fewer hypotheses to avoid multiple testing, or assess larger effect sizes that are economically meaningful before committing capital.

## Appendix: Hypothesis Evolution
1. Original (iteration 1): "S&P 500 rises in January every year."  
   - Rationale for refinement: Too broad and implausible; historically falsified by many years and not actionable.

2. Iteration 2: "Mean January return of S&P 500 > 0 (unconditional), test with one‑sample t."  
   - Reason refined: Unconditional test insensitive to conditional patterns and subject to seasonality/noise; effect size small and would likely be economically immaterial.

3. Iteration 3: "Mean January return > 0 conditional on prior December negative (Dec < 0)."  
   - Reason refined: Introduced conditioning to capture potential reversal/mean‑reversion after down Decembers; exploratory thresholds showed sensitivity and small conditional cell sizes.

4. Final (iteration 4): "Pooled mean January total return (SPY & ^GSPC) > 0 conditional on prior Dec <= −1.5%, tested with one‑sided permutation/bootstrap and bootstrap 95% CI; require pre‑specified 5‑yr OOS holdout and accept only if pooled conditional n ≥ 30 and test power ≥ 80% to detect 1.0% monthly effect."  
   - Reason: Added specific threshold (−1.5%), pooled series to increase observations, enforced robustness (bootstrap, OOS), and hard acceptance criteria (sample size and power) to avoid overfitting. Final test failed these acceptance criteria (n = 23 < 30 and p non‑significant), leading to the WEAK verdict.
